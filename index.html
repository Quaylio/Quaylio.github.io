Market Analysis

The Singapore-India Coal Route: Why It Remains Dry Bulk's Most Active Corridor

By Editorial Board October 12, 2025 5 Min Read

The global trade of thermal coal relies on predictable tonnage, and nowhere is this more apparent than the corridor connecting Indonesian loading ports, routed via Singapore, into the vast discharge hubs of the Indian subcontinent. With over 100 active vessel operators and 500 brokers working the lane, the Singapore-India route continues to define spot market behavior across the region.

Despite macroeconomic shifts toward alternative energy, the baseline demand for seaborne thermal coal remains rigid. For dry bulk operators, this route offers reliable employment for Supramax and Panamax tonnage. However, the density of traffic brings fierce competition and rate volatility.

The paradox of the Singapore-India corridor is that while there is always cargo, capturing an optimal freight rate requires flawless timing and exceptional market intelligence.

Brokers positioned in Singapore report that fixture cycles are compressing. Where previously a charterer might float a cargo requirement days in advance, the current spot market operates on a timeline of mere hours. If a vessel owner misses the initial circulation of a cargo, the positional advantage is lost to a competitor.

As we look to Q4, the pressure on this corridor will only increase as monsoon seasons recede and winter stockpiling demands accelerate. Owners and charterers alike must prepare for a tighter tonnage list and corresponding rate spikes.

Regulatory Impact

The Hidden Cost of CII: Why Older Tonnage is Vanishing from the Atlantic

By Editorial Board June 19, 2026 4 Min Read

The implementation of the Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) was intended to gradually decarbonize the global fleet. Instead, it has abruptly fractured the Atlantic dry bulk market into a two-tier system, leaving charterers scrambling for compliant tonnage while older vessels flee to less regulated corridors.

Our analysis of Q2 fixture data shows a stark migration. Supramax vessels over 15 years old, which historically carried a significant portion of US Gulf grain to Europe, are disappearing from these routes. Major charterers with strict ESG mandates are outright refusing vessels with "D" or "E" CII ratings, regardless of the freight discount offered.

We are watching a geographic segregation of the fleet in real-time. The Atlantic is becoming a premium pool for modern eco-vessels, while older tonnage is being pushed into the Pacific and Indian Oceans where emissions scrutiny is currently lower.

For ship owners, the math is becoming brutal. Retrofitting a 2008-built Panamax with energy-saving devices to improve its CII rating often exceeds the asset's residual commercial value. Consequently, owners are choosing to deploy these ships on longer, slower voyages in the East to artificially improve their carbon metrics per nautical mile.

Unless European and North American charterers are willing to pay a sustained premium for top-tier eco-tonnage, the Atlantic basin will face a localized capacity squeeze by the end of the year.

In Conversation With

A Singapore-Based Freight Broker on the Future of Spot Markets

Subject: Senior Broker, Dry Bulk Division — 15 Years in the Industry
Conducted by Editorial Board October 15, 2025

Editor's Note: To encourage candid discussion about the realities of modern freight brokerage, this interview was conducted under the condition of anonymity. The subject currently manages a desk handling Supramax and Panamax fixtures in the Pacific basin.

Quaylio Insights: You've been fixing ships for fifteen years. How has the daily process of matching cargo to tonnage changed over that time?
Fundamentally, the cargo is the same, but the speed of information has accelerated exponentially. It used to be that you had a day to work a firm order. Now, if a cargo comes onto the market, every competitive desk in Singapore, London, and Dubai has it in minutes. The premium is no longer on possessing the information; the premium is on the relationship required to execute the fixture before someone else does.
Quaylio Insights: What is the biggest inefficiency you see in spot markets today?
Empty legs and partial utilization. We still see owners ballasting ships across oceans on pure speculation because they lack visibility into localized cargo availability. Or worse, fixing a vessel at 80% capacity because they couldn't find a top-off cargo in time. The margins in dry bulk are too thin to be sailing empty holds, yet it happens daily.
Quaylio Insights: Where does the market go from here? Will we see structural changes in how brokers operate?
Brokers aren't going anywhere; the advisory layer is critical when millions of dollars and physical assets are on the line. But the "postman" broker—the guy who just forwards emails from charterers to owners without adding intelligence—is dead. The future belongs to brokers who can instantly synthesize market positions and bring actionable, qualified tonnage to the table instantly.
